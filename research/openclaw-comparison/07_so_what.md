# So-What Summary: OpenClaw vs WorkTrain

OpenClaw (https://github.com/openclaw/openclaw) is a self-hosted personal AI assistant platform with 369k stars, daily shipping velocity, and infrastructure subsystems (pluggable context engine, SQLite task registry, unified trajectory schema, service audit) that WorkTrain is building in parallel at a much smaller scale. OpenClaw is not a direct competitive threat today -- their VISION explicitly rejects the coordinator pipeline model that is WorkTrain's core, and their users hire them to collapse messaging-channel fragmentation, not to run overnight dev pipelines -- but the distribution gap is real and a `skills/coding-agent/` directory already exists in their repo, meaning a pivot to "good enough" autonomous coding is technically trivial and would reach orders of magnitude more users than WorkTrain can organically. The single most actionable switching driver is WorkTrain's silent-failure gap: `worktrain daemon --start` reports success on immediate crash, there is no `worktrain doctor` command, and operators have no plain-English status briefing -- three usability failures that OpenClaw's service-audit/doctor-fix pattern directly solves and that give operators a concrete reason to look elsewhere. The recommended decision (decision ledger, `urgent` rows) is to immediately adopt three OpenClaw-derived patterns: a `worktrain doctor` command with typed service-audit issue codes, a unified trajectory event schema to replace the two-store split, and a pluggable ContextEngine interface to replace the hardcoded `buildSystemPrompt()` pipeline. The single piece of new information that would most change this read is the contents of OpenClaw's `skills/coding-agent/` directory: if it contains multi-phase step enforcement logic rather than a flat agent prompt, the competitive timeline compresses from "watch closely" to "act now."
